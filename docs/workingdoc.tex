\begin{abstract}
This proposal outlines a data mining project focused on the 2017 Goodreads dataset. This study aims to discover relationships between book genres, user behaviors, and how readers structure their reviews. We will be applying different techniques such as Association Rule Mining, K-Means Clustering, and Latent Dirichlet Allocation. This proposal seeks to understand how readers associate different genres in an attempt to define different segments of the reading community.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
Data Mining, Goodreads, Association Rules, Clustering, Text Mining, Pattern Discovery
\end{IEEEkeywords}

\section{Introduction}
The digital age has transformed reading from a personal, solo activity into a social activity. Various platforms like Goodreads and Fable provide a massive database of user-generated data through ratings, reviews, and custom shelves. While many similar projects exist to predict user's rating there is more value in understanding how users engage with literature. This project focuses on the UCSD Goodreads dataset to find these patterns.

\section{Description}
This project will utilize the Goodreads Book Datasets (2017) curated by Julian McAuley at UCSD, Rishabh Misra and Ndapap Nakashole

\subsection{A. Source and Scale }
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Source:} \url{https://cseweb.ucsd.edu/~jmcauley/datasets/goodreads.html}
    \item \textbf{Size:} The full dataset contains metadata for 2.36 million books and 15.7 million detailed review texts. For this project, a subset (e.g., the "Young Adult" or "History" category) will be utilized to maintain computational feasibility while ensuring a sample size of over 100,000 records.
    \item \textbf{Format:} Data is provided in the CSV and json format, requiring significant preprocessing and flattening.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Key Features and Schema}
The dataset is split into two primary components:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Metadata (\textit{goodreads\_books.json}):} Contains \texttt{book\_id}, \texttt{title}, \texttt{authors}, \texttt{average\_rating}, and importantly, \texttt{popular\_shelves} (user-defined tags).

    \item \textbf{Reviews (\textit{goodreads\_reviews.json}):} Contains \texttt{user\_id}, \texttt{book\_id}, \texttt{rating}, \texttt{review\_text}, and \texttt{n\_votes}.
    \item \textbf{User Interactions (\textit{goodreads\_interactions.csv}):} Contains \texttt{user\_id}, \texttt{book\_id}, \texttt{is\_read}, \texttt{rating}, and \texttt{is\_reviewed}.
\end{enumerate}


\section{C. Data Quality and Preprocessing}

Known issues include many user-defined shelves that share common tags like "to-read" and varying review lengths. This is most likely caused from Goodread's ability to easily add books to user's "to-read" shelve. Preprocessing will involve tokenization, stop-word removal for text mining and transforming the nested JSON shelves into a transaction-style for association mining.

\section{Discovery Questions}

This project moves beyond prediction models to investigate the following questions the team had:
\begin{itemize}
    \item What are the hidden associations between specific book genres and user-defined "shelves"? (Do readers of "Romantasy" (Romance Fantasy) also show high interest in other genres like fantasy?
    \item Can we identify distinct Reader Personas based on interaction metrics? By clustering users based on their average rating variance, review frequency, and review length our goal is to discover if it is possible to classify readers into different groups?
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Association Rule Mining (FP-Growth)}\label{AA}
    We will treat each user's shelves as a transaction. Using the FP-Growth Algorithm to discover rules with high lift and confidence to identify non-obvious genre relationships. Our goal is to move beyond the standard "Fantasy" label and discover more sub-genre patterns.
\subsection{Clustering (K-Means)}
    To answer the second discovery question, we will apply K-Means clustering on standarized numerical features derived the user dataset. We will be using the "Elbow Method" and "Silhoutette Score" to determine the optimal number of reader segments.
\subsection{Text Mining (LDA - Topic Modeling)}
    Using the Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) algorithm, we will perform topic modeling on the \texttt{review\_text}. This will allow us to discover the primary topics of discussion within different genres without pre-defining what those topics are.

\subsection{Preprocessing and Data Transformation}

In order to transition from the introductory and theoretical questions from the previous sections our team implemented multiple scripts to select and gather the raw data from the dataset.
 To begin answering our discovery questions we had to preprocess our data through a filter the massive dataset by cleaning some administrative tags and filtering out non-English languages. First we performed a keyword search across the "popular\_shelves" attribute of the \texttt{goodreads\_books.json.gz} file. We specifically search for genres: 'romantasy', 'romantic-fantasy', 'fantasy-romance', 'romance', 'fantasy', and 'romantic'. The filtered results are stored in \texttt{01\_filteredbooks.json}. The resulting json consists of records: book\_id, title, average\_rating, shelves\_list.


To answer discovery question 1, we need to examine the different user interactions, so we implemented a second filtering script utilizing the book\_id from our previous script to extract reviews from the \texttt{goodreads\_reviews\_dedup.json.gz} dataset, storing the output in \texttt{03\_extractedreviews.csv}.

\subsection{Answering Discovery Question 1: Genre Associations}

Our team originally proposed if readers of ``Romantasy'' (Romance Fantasy) also showed high interest in other specific romance sub-genres. During our initial run developing our implementation for Milestone 2 our FP-Growth algorithm generated relationships based off structural administrative tags rather than genre relationships like romance. Furthermore, variations in user-generated text caused our target genre to be heavily diluted. To correct this for Milestone 3, we implemented a data normalization pipeline to aggregate variations of Romantasy (e.g., ``fantasy romance'', ``paranormal romance'') into standardized categories. We also significantly lowered our algorithm parameters, dropping the minimum support threshold to 0.01 (1\% of transactions) and the minimum lift threshold to 2, to ensure we were not filtering out niche sub-genre connections.

\subsection{Discovery Question 2: Reader Personas}

The second discovery question explored whether our team could categorize users as "critical reviewers" or "casual consumers" based on average rating variance, review frequency, and review length. To categorize these users, our team performed a K-Means analysis using Elbow charts to determine the optimal number of clusters based on \{review\_frequency, avg\_rating, rating\_variance\}.

Based on the Elbow method, we determined that four clusters (Table \ref{tab:persona_profiles}) best represent the Goodreads user base: "Generous Consumer," "Harsh Consumer," "Powered Reader," and "Standard Reviewer."

\subsubsection{Cluster 0: Generous Consumer}

This is the biggest reading group. Although the readers in this group only rate books occasionally, they seem to love the books they do rate as they have an average rating of 4.44 with a small rating variance of 0.56.

\subsubsection{Cluster 1: Harsh Consumer }
This group is the third largest reading group however this group also seems to read the least. This reading group is categorized by the lowest overall rating of any group. With an average rating of 2.94 and a high variance, it suggests that they either find a rare "5-star" gem or more likely loathe what they read. These are your more difficult to please critics.

\subsubsection{Cluster 2: Powered reader}

This is the smallest reading group. However despite being the smallest this group averages a review every single day and review the most books out of any group with a review frequency of 364.22. They are fair but tend to be firm in their ratings with a rating variance of 1.08 but only an average rating of 3.77.

\subsubsection{Cluster 3: Standard reviewer}

This is the second biggest reading group. They tend to be more active than the Casual consumers but are more grounded in reviews. This group is pretty standard across the entire board as their average rating of 3.44 shows the books the group reads doesn't leave much of an impact on them. While this group does review a lot of books with a review frequency of 26.12 they tend to rate books uniquely with a rating variance of 1.47.

\subsection{Discovery Question 3: Engagement Volume}

Our team's final discovery question asked what patterns emerge from highly up-voted reviews compared to reviews with a lot of down-votes or no votes at all. To answer this, we extracted user engagement metrics from the \texttt{goodreads\_reviews\_dedup.json.gz} dataset to identify behavioral patterns.
A Pearson correlation analysis reveals a notable moderate positive correlation of $0.52$ between the number of comments (\texttt{n\_comments}) and the number of votes (\texttt{n\_votes}), suggesting that reviews sparking discussion are also more likely to be perceived as helpful or popular. Conversely, our initial hypothesis that \texttt{review\_length} would drive higher engagement was unsupported, as it maintained negligible correlations with both \texttt{n\_votes} ($0.03$) and \texttt{n\_comments} ($0.04$). Furthermore, the numerical \texttt{rating} appears entirely decoupled from interaction volume, showing correlations of $0.00$ and $-0.01$ against votes and comments, respectively. These results indicate that while social interactions (voting and commenting) tend to scale together, they are driven by factors other than the sentiment of the rating or the verbosity of the review.

\subsubsection{Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) }

Because our Milestone 3 findings proved that longer reviews and star ratings do not correlate with higher community engagement, our team decided to transition to qualitative text mining to answer our final discovery question. We utilized Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA), This is a machine learning algorithm designed to tokenize a massive dataset to find patterns and divide them up.

Our team first had to prepare our review dataset. Importing our \texttt{03\_extractedreviews.csv} into a Pandas dataframe, we tokenized the review text while simultaneously stripping non-alphabetic characters and filtering out custom domain stop-words (e.g., `book', `read', `story') that add no value. After converting our cleaned tokens into a matrix, we built an LDA model. To ensure we extracted concise and highly specific themes, we configured the model to search for 40 distinct components ($n\_components=40$). We then mapped these generated topics against our engagement metrics (\texttt{n\_votes}, \texttt{n\_comments}, and \texttt{review\_count}).

Reviewing our modeled data allows us to identify several distinct themes that drive community interaction. While our team identified 40 topics a few of the big themes were Analytical critique, Emotional Reactionaires and Administrative / ARC disclaimers.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Analytical critique} was found in themes 4 and 36 these topics mention specifically world building and pacing of the books. These books averaged about 1.27-2.95 votes and 0.4-1.06 comments. This suggests that the analytical critique reviews are popular with the goodreads community and are favored greatly.
     \item \textbf{Emotional Reactionaries (Topics 19 \& 27):} These topics captured reviews focused entirely on feelings, specifically mentioning ``book hangovers'' and crying. Essentially, the readers who wrote these reviews loved the books so much they could not seem to get over the ending. Interestingly, these reviews saw notably lower engagement than the analytical critiques, averaging between 0.82 and 0.91 votes, and 0.25 to 0.51 comments.
    \item \textbf{Administrative / ARC disclaimers (Topic 30):} Topic 30 was an interesting one. Despite our team doing our best to pre-process our datasets and remove administrative tags a few reviews were prevalent enough to be included. Topic 30 mainly noted that the readers talked about their blog or disclosing advanced reader's copies before reviewing. Most authors hand out Advanced Reader's Copies (ARC) for them to get a bunch of reviews before a product launch. Since the expectation for readers with ARCs is to review books our team expects to have high levels of engagement. Furthermore, this group had a \texttt{n\_votes} value of 1.90 while \texttt{n\_comments} had a value of 0.54.

\end{itemize}
